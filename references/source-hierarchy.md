# Source Hierarchy and Evidence Rules

Use this file when selecting sources or deciding whether evidence is sufficient.

## Core Source Rule

Only cite a source if you can access it and provide a clickable link, title or organization, year, and a one-sentence conclusion. Marketing pages, seller pages, KOL posts, social media anecdotes, and unverifiable expert quotes are not evidence.

## Tier 1: Highest-Priority International Sources

Systematic reviews and indexed research:

- Cochrane Reviews
- PubMed / NIH / NLM, prioritizing systematic reviews and meta-analyses
- NEJM, The Lancet, JAMA, BMJ, and other peer-reviewed journals when directly relevant

Institutions, regulators, and guidelines:

- WHO
- FDA, EMA, MHRA
- CDC, ECDC, NHS, NICE
- USDA, EFSA

Clinical decision support and public medical education:

- UpToDate, DynaMed, BMJ Best Practice when accessible
- Merck / MSD Manual
- Mayo Clinic, Cleveland Clinic, Johns Hopkins, Harvard Health

Evidence aggregation and consumer testing:

- Examine.com for supplements and nutrition, especially when it cites primary literature
- Science-Based Medicine and Quackwatch for pseudoscience claims
- Consumer Reports, Which?, Wirecutter, or equivalent independent consumer testing for product execution and comparative performance

## Tier 2: Chinese Authoritative Sources

- 国家卫健委
- 国家药监局 (NMPA), including drug labels and special food databases
- 市场监管总局
- 中国营养学会 and 《中国居民膳食指南》
- 中华医学会 and specialty society guidelines
- 默沙东诊疗手册中文版
- 丁香医生 / 丁香园 when professional review and source trail are available
- 果壳网 / 科学松鼠会 as science communication starting points, but trace important claims to primary or institutional sources
- 中国消费者协会 comparative testing reports

## Tier 3: Use Cautiously

- Wikipedia: use only as a starting point; cite the original source it references, not Wikipedia itself.
- Single studies not yet incorporated into reviews: mention as limited evidence, not consensus.
- Brand-sponsored studies: use only with clear conflict-of-interest caveat and never as the sole support for 🟢.

## Not Evidence

- Ecommerce pages, seller copy, ads, influencer/KOL claims, 小红书/抖音/公众号 personal experience
- "某某医生说" without a findable source
- Testimonials, before-after photos, and star ratings
- "祖传", "古方", "千年验证", or "天然无副作用" claims
- Mechanism diagrams from the brand that are not backed by independent evidence

## Evidence Sufficiency

Use the strictest standard for claims with direct medical consequences.

- Medical treatment/prevention claims usually need systematic reviews, RCTs, clinical guidelines, or strong institutional consensus.
- Nutrition, supplement, and skincare claims need human evidence for the specific outcome, dose, population, and form. Mechanism-only evidence is usually 🟠.
- Physical products can be 🟢 when the mechanism is established and the product execution is verified by standards, certifications, independent tests, or transparent specifications.
- Multiple Tier 1/2 sources with the same position can be enough for 🟢 or 🔴 even when no product-specific RCT exists.
- If authorities disagree in a way that affects the answer, use 🟠 and explain the disagreement.
- If you cannot access enough authoritative sources, use ⚪ and state what could not be verified.

## Search Patterns

For each mechanism, search at least three angles:

- English academic: `<mechanism> systematic review`, `<ingredient> randomized trial`, `<condition> guideline <intervention>`.
- Institution/regulator: `site:who.int`, `site:fda.gov`, `site:cdc.gov`, `site:nhs.uk`, `site:nice.org.uk`, `site:efsa.europa.eu`.
- Chinese authority/professional: `国家卫健委 <claim>`, `国家药监局 <ingredient/product>`, `中国营养学会 <nutrient>`, `丁香医生 <claim>`.

When the user asks about a specific brand or model, search both:

- Mechanism evidence: what the technology/ingredient can do in principle.
- Execution evidence: dosage, concentration, wavelength, certification, independent testing, recall/safety notices, and product specifications.
